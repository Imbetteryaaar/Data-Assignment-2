Australia's Wild Quarter

A young country has quietly placed almost a quarter of its land and half of its ocean under protection. This is the story of how, and where the gaps remain.

Introduction

Protecting a continent, one park at a time

National parks are where Australians meet their own country: the desert ranges, rainforest gorges, alpine meadows and rugged coasts that define the place. They are also a promise a decision to keep parts of the continent wild for the species that live there and the people who come after us.

That promise has grown remarkably fast. In 2004, barely a tenth of Australia's land was protected. Today it is more than a fifth, and the country has pledged to reach 30% by 2030. Yet the green is spread unevenly: some states shelter nearly half their land, others less than a tenth.

This visualisation follows that green across the map, back through time, and into the parks themselves who looks after them, who visits them, and what they are racing to save.

Part One · The Map

A continent of uneven green

Protected land is not shared equally. The Australian Capital Territory leads the nation, with 56% of its small footprint protected, followed by Tasmania (43%) and the vast, arid west and centre. The fertile, heavily farmed east tells the opposite story: Queensland and New South Wales each protect under 11% of their land.

Hover over any state to read its exact figure. The pattern is clear: the more a landscape is wanted for farming and cities, the harder it is to protect.

Part Two · The Climb

Two decades of rapid protection

The growth has been steep. Since 2004 the protected share of Australia's land has doubled, while marine protection leapt even faster from a tenth of the ocean to almost half. A run of large additions in 2012 and again after 2020 reshaped the seas around the continent.

On land, the climb has been steadier. At 22.6% in 2024, Australia sits a little over halfway to its 2030 target of protecting 30% of its landmass.

Part Three · The Custodians

Who is doing the protecting?

Behind the headline numbers is a quieter shift. Much of the new protected land is not a conventional national park at all, but an Indigenous Protected Area Country managed by Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander groups according to their own objectives, and counted as part of the National Reserve System.

In 2004 these areas covered just 2% of the continent. Twenty years on they cover 11%, the single largest driver of Australia's expanding reserve system. Each shaded band traces one biennial CAPAD release, updated every two years, giving a detailed picture of how and when the expansion happened.

Part Four · The Giants

The ten largest national parks

Australia's biggest parks are almost unimaginably large. Munga-Thirri–Simpson Desert National Park in South Australia covers 36,000 km² bigger than Belgium. Kakadu, the country's best-known wilderness, is a distant second.

Notice where they sit. The giants are clustered in the arid interior and the tropical north, far from the coastal cities exactly the land that few people want for anything else.

Part Five · The History

A protection habit, more than a century old

Australia took to national parks early. Royal National Park, south of Sydney, was reserved in 1879 the second national park anywhere in the world, after Yellowstone. A scatter of others followed before 1920.

But the real surge came much later. The environmental movement of the 1970s and 1980s turned a habit into a wave: more well-known parks were established in the 1980s than in any decade before or since.

Part Six · The Visitors

A nation that loves its parks

Protected land is not locked away. Australians pour into it. New South Wales alone recorded a remarkable 65.6 million domestic visits to its national parks in 2024–25, a new record. But the road there was turbulent: the Black Summer bushfires (2019–20) and COVID-19 lockdowns slashed visits to barely half the normal rate, before a steady recovery led to back-to-back records.

Visitors are not only locals: almost three-quarters of overseas tourists to NSW now spend time in a national park. And the crowds concentrate. Four of the five busiest parks sit on Sydney's doorstep.

Part Seven · The Icons

The long road back to 2019

Not every famous park is booming. The Northern Territory's two flagship parks (both jointly managed with their Traditional Owners and both on the World Heritage list) tell a story of COVID collapse and uneven recovery. To compare them fairly despite their different sizes, each park's visitor numbers are indexed to its own pre-COVID (2019) peak: 100% means a full return to 2019 levels.

Both crashed below a third of normal in 2021. From there their paths split. Uluru-Kata Tjuta clawed back to 97% as domestic road-trips returned. Kakadu's recovery stalled at 83% and even slipped in 2024, reflecting its heavier reliance on overseas drive tourists who have been slower to come back. For remote parks that live on long-haul visitors, these swings shape the budgets and jobs of whole communities.

Part Eight · The Reason

What the parks are racing to save

In the end, protected areas exist for the living things inside them. Australia is one of the most biologically distinctive places on Earth, and one of the most under pressure. More than 2,100 species are now listed as threatened under national environment law.

Plants make up the largest share of that list, with 1,402 species across all threat levels. But the losses cut across every major group: mammals, birds, amphibians, fish, reptiles, and invertebrates. Every well-placed park is a piece of the safety net.

In Closing

Halfway up the mountain

Australia's protected estate is a genuine national achievement, doubled in a generation, increasingly led by Indigenous custodians, and loved by tens of millions of visitors a year. But the map still has thin spots exactly where life is busiest, and the 30%-by-2030 target is not yet won.

The wild quarter is not a finished thing. It is a promise still being kept.

About this visualisation

This data story was created for FIT2179 Data Visualisation 2 at Monash University. It uses the Vega-Lite grammar of graphics for every chart and map. Each chart's JSON specification is published, human-readable, in the /charts folder of this repository; the map geometry is in /data.

Why

Australians care deeply about national parks but rarely see the national picture: how much is protected, where, and how fast it is changing. This visualisation makes that picture visible.

Who

Designed for a general Australian audience: residents, travellers and students curious about the country's natural places. No specialist or statistical knowledge is assumed.

What

Real, recent, public data combining two main sources: the Collaborative Australian Protected Areas Database (CAPAD) and national-park visitor statistics, along with biodiversity and park-history data.

How

Munzer's framework guided the idioms: choropleth and proportional-symbol maps for spatial tasks, lines and areas for trends, and bar, lollipop and slope charts for comparison and change.

Data sources

  • ABS: Protected areas (2025), based on CAPAD
  • DCCEEW: Collaborative Australian Protected Areas Database (CAPAD)
  • NSW NPWS: Record visits to NSW national parks (2025)
  • Parks Australia & state park agencies: park areas, history and visitor data
  • DCCEEW: Threatened species under the EPBC Act
  • State boundary geometry: world-geojson (Natural Earth derived), simplified by the author

Method & tools

All charts and maps were built with Vega-Lite 5. The map uses a conic equal-area projection appropriate for Australia. Boundary data was simplified to keep the page fast to load.

Each Vega-Lite specification is available, formatted for human readability, in this GitHub repository.
